## The book

<p>
  <i>The Greatest Shortcoming</i> traces a hundred years of American
  demographic and environmental governance &mdash; from the Olmsted Report's
  grammar of scarcity to climate triage as administrative routine. It
  identifies the actors who built the instruments, the organizations that
  circulated them, the metaphors that made them persuasive, and the political
  projects they served. And it asks whether measurement can be redirected:
  whether the same quantitative tools that narrowed the circle of concern can
  be rebuilt to widen it.
  <span class="draft-tag" title="Draft copy — author review pending">draft</span>
</p>

<p>
  The book addresses four scholarly communities whose debates converge on the
  problem it analyzes: <i>science and technology studies</i>, <i>critical and
  historical demography</i>, <i>environmental sociology and political
  ecology</i>, and <i>extremism studies and the sociology of far-right
  movements</i>. The full statement of how the book speaks to each of them is
  in the <a href="{{ '/chapters/preface/' | relative_url }}">preface</a>.
</p>

## The author

<p>
  Brian C. Keegan is a computational social scientist trained as a mechanical
  engineer, then in quantitative communication studies, with detours through
  political science and business. He works in the discipline of misfit toys
  known as information science, studying digital traces of social behavior
  from online communities like Wikipedia, Reddit, and Twitter. This book is a
  return to his STS undergraduate roots after almost two decades in the
  theories and methods of other disciplines.
