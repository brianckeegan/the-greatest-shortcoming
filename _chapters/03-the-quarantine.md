abstract: |
  After the 1994 Cairo Conference on Population and Development delegitimized
  coercive population restriction in mainstream development discourse,
  population politics did not disappear. It went through a quarantine. *The
  Quarantine* traces how a Michigan ophthalmologist, John Tanton, built a
  parallel institutional infrastructure — the Federation for American
  Immigration Reform, the Center for Immigration Studies, NumbersUSA, and the
  affiliated journals and think tanks — that reframed restriction in the
  fiscal-impact vocabulary of policy expertise. The chapter dissects the
  1993 Huddle study (&ldquo;immigrants cost \$42.5 billion&rdquo;) as the
  era&rsquo;s exemplary instrument, showing how cross-tabulation became a
  political technology and how *misaggregation* — the strategic choice of
  what to count, over what time horizon, and at what jurisdictional level —
  produced predetermined findings in formally race-neutral language. It closes
  on the Sierra Club's 1998 board verdict on immigration and on what the
  chapter calls *the architecture of ignorance*: an institutional arrangement
  that survived delegitimation by embedding itself in the aesthetic of
  credentialed policy analysis.

<p class="aside"><i>From &sect;What an Immigrant Costs: The Impact Statement and Its Arithmetic, mid-chapter:</i></p>

<p>A simplified illustration makes the mechanism visible. Take a state with one million immigrants. Suppose 800,000 are working-age adults who pay more in taxes than they consume in services; 100,000 are children enrolled in public schools whose education costs exceed their household&rsquo;s direct fiscal contribution in the current year; and 100,000 are elderly individuals drawing on Medicaid or other public benefits. Aggregate the full population and calculate the net fiscal effect: the working-age majority&rsquo;s tax contributions offset the costs associated with the young and old, producing a figure near fiscal neutrality. Now decompose. Isolate the elderly subgroup, calculate its fiscal impact separately, and report that subgroup&rsquo;s net cost as a headline finding. The same one million people have become a fiscal burden, because the analyst chose to present the part rather than the whole. Add a jurisdictional filter: the elderly immigrants are concentrated in three counties, and their Medicaid costs fall on those counties&rsquo; budgets. A county-level impact analysis of those three counties would show a dramatic fiscal burden, even though the statewide analysis shows none. The data have not changed. The aggregation choice has changed, and the aggregation choice has changed the finding.</p>

<p>The \$42.5 billion figure from the Huddle study had a further property that made it politically potent: it could be divided. Divide total cost by the number of immigrants and the result is a cost-per-immigrant, a single figure that attaches a dollar value to a human life. The cost-per-immigrant is the Quarantine era&rsquo;s equivalent of the doubling time. Both are single numbers that compress a complex population-level phenomenon into a portable, citeable, reproducible figure. Both are alarming by design. Both suppress the conditions under which the number was produced. And both convert a conditional finding (if you count only costs, in this jurisdiction, over this time frame, you get this number) into an unconditional claim (immigrants cost this much).</p>

<p>The number won because the number fit the sentence. A legislator can say &ldquo;immigrants cost taxpayers \$X per year&rdquo; in a floor speech. A legislator cannot say &ldquo;the fiscal impact of immigration varies by education level, legal status, state of residence, time since arrival, age at entry, household composition, and time horizon, and the net effect depends on which of these dimensions you aggregate and which you decompose.&rdquo;</p>
