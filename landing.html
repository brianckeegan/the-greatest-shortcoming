Picture a bottle.

At eleven o'clock…

…drop in a single bacterium.

It divides once a minute — one becomes two.

Two become four.

For most of the hour, the bottle looks nearly empty.

11:55 — three percent full.

11:56 — the fill doubles.

Six percent.

11:57 — and doubles again.

Twelve percent.

11:58 — a quarter full.

11:59 — half full.

Noon. The bottle is full.

12:01 — it begins to spill out.

12:03 — the circles fill the screen.

"The greatest shortcoming of the human race is our inability to understand the exponential function."

— Albert Bartlett, 1972

For over half a century,

Al Bartlett's bacteria-in-a-bottle thought experiment

influenced a movement of scientists and environmental activists worried about

overpopulation.

When Bartlett died in 2013,

he was eulogized in white nationalist journals

for his advocacy to end immigration into the United States.

This is the story of how quantitative tools and arguments about conservation

repeatedly become tools of exclusion and dominance.

Quantitative Chauvinism

how the numbers seize power

A style of reasoning that hands a single metric jurisdiction over political judgment — collapsing messy social complexity into one aggregated trajectory, then sealing the result inside portable artifacts that travel as if they were neutral facts. It is the use of numerical form to close political questions before they can be opened, to present a simplification as the whole picture, and to reframe dissent as a failure to understand the data.

political register

Lebensraum Imaginaries

what the numbers are recruited to justify

A recurring architecture — narratives of the future, organizational habits, rhetorical moves — through which ecological emergency is converted into demographic hierarchy, and coercive governance is presented as the reluctant, responsible management of natural limits. Named for Friedrich Ratzel's Lebensraum, a concept that crossed from biology into the territorial doctrine of the Third Reich — and whose afterlives remain recognizable from the Cold War to the coming climate century.
