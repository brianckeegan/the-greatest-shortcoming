## Overview

*The Greatest Shortcoming* argues that Al Bartlett's celebrated lecture "Arithmetic, Population and Energy" — and the broader neo-Malthusian tradition it represents — functioned not merely as scientific communication but as a politically consequential ideology that shaped land use, housing policy, and environmental governance in Boulder, Colorado and beyond.

## Central Claim

Bartlett's exponential growth arguments, while mathematically valid in the abstract, were systematically deployed to oppose urban density, affordable housing, and demographic growth in ways that materially benefited affluent homeowners while excluding low-income residents and communities of color. This application of ecological limits rhetoric to urban politics constitutes a form of **ecofascism**: the use of environmental language to naturalize and justify social exclusion.

## Key Claims

1. **The rhetoric of limits is not neutral.** Bartlett's framing of growth as inherently catastrophic provided scientific legitimacy to political positions that served specific class and racial interests, regardless of his personal intentions.

2. **Boulder is a case study in Malthusian urbanism.** Decades of growth restriction, enforced in the name of environmental carrying capacity, have produced one of the most unaffordable cities in the United States — a city whose progressivism coexists with radical socioeconomic exclusion.

3. **Neo-Malthusianism has roots in eugenics and nativism.** The intellectual lineage running from Malthus through Paul Ehrlich to Bartlett's allies in FAIR (Federation for American Immigration Reform) and related organizations reveals a persistent entanglement of population thinking with racial exclusion.

4. **Computational methods reveal rhetorical patterns invisible to traditional scholarship.** Text analysis of Bartlett's corpus and the policy documents it influenced exposes recurring tropes, citation networks, and discursive strategies that are difficult to identify through close reading alone.

5. **Alternatives exist.** Pro-housing and eco-modernist frameworks demonstrate that environmental sustainability and social equity are compatible goals — and that the either/or framing of Malthusian urbanism is a political choice, not an ecological necessity.

## Significance

This project contributes to three overlapping debates:

- **Urban history and housing politics:** Understanding the ideological roots of growth restriction is essential for contemporary housing reform efforts in the American West.
- **Science and society:** Bartlett's case illuminates how scientific authority can be mobilized for political ends, and how the distinction between "just doing math" and advocacy collapses in practice.
- **Critical environmental humanities:** The book advances a critique of limits-based environmentalism from within the left, arguing for a more expansive and solidaristic ecological politics.
