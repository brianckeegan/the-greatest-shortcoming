Picture a bottle.

At eleven o'clock…

…drop in a single bacterium.

It divides once a minute — one becomes two.

Two become four.

For most of the hour, the bottle looks nearly empty.

11:55 — three percent full.

11:56 — the fill doubles.

Six percent.

11:57 — and doubles again.

Twelve percent.

11:58 — a quarter full.

11:59 — half full.

Noon. The bottle is full.

12:01 — it begins to spill out.

12:03 — the circles fill the screen.

"The greatest shortcoming of the human race is our inability to understand the exponential function."

— Albert Bartlett, 1972

For over half a century, Al Bartlett's bacteria-in-a-bottle thought experiment influenced a movement of scientists and environmental activists worried about overpopulation.

When Bartlett died in 2013, he was eulogized in white nationalist journals for his advocacy to end immigration into the United States.

This is the story of how quantitative tools and arguments about conservation repeatedly become tools of exclusion and dominance.
